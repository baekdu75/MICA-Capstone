The Myth of "EVs Are Always Cheaper"

Electric vehicles are often marketed as universally cheaper to operate than gas-powered cars. The pitch is simple and appealing: electricity costs less than gasoline, so you'll save money every time you drive.

But this narrative glosses over a critical truth: the real cost depends heavily on where you live. A single national average doesn't capture the dramatic differences in electricity and gasoline prices across the United States. What's cheap in one state might be expensive in another.

2

Energy Costs Vary Wildly by State

When you look at the data, one pattern becomes immediately clear: electricity and gas prices change dramatically across state lines. Some states offer incredibly cheap electricity, making EV charging a bargain. Others have high electricity rates that diminish the cost advantage significantly.

The same volatility applies to gasoline. While everyone experiences price fluctuations at the pump, the baseline price per gallon varies considerably by region due to state taxes, local regulations, and distribution costs.

Insurance Complicates the Story

Even when energy costs favor EVs, there's another expense that often gets overlooked in basic comparisons: insurance premiums.

EV insurance tends to run higher than comparable gas or hybrid vehicles. The reasons are straightforward: specialized parts, expensive battery repairs, and newer technology mean higher replacement and repair costs. Insurance companies price policies accordingly.
